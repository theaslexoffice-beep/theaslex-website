Verizon v. ACIT: ITAT Delhi Validates the Limited Risk Model
and Settles Three Transfer Pricing Battles

A detailed analysis of ITA No. 442/DEL/2017 (AY 2012-13) — the Tribunal's treatment of the Hub-and-Spoke telecom model, Berry Ratio as PLI, telecom charges as non-royalty, and Section 80-IA eligibility for NLD/ILD services.

On 25 February 2026, the Delhi Bench "I" of the Income Tax Appellate Tribunal delivered its order in Verizon Communications India Pvt. Ltd. v. Additional CIT, Special Range-9, New Delhi (ITA No. 442/DEL/2017, AY 2012-13). The case touched upon four distinct but interconnected issues — each of which carries significant implications for multinational enterprises operating in India under hub-and-spoke business models.

The assessee was represented by Shri Ajay Vohra, Senior Advocate, along with S/Shri Alok Kr. Sinha, Devashish Poddar, Ms. Nupur Jindal & Ms. Hemlata (Chartered Accountants). The Revenue was represented by Shri Dharm Veer Singh, CIT-DR.

The Facts: Verizon's Indian Operations

Verizon Communications India Pvt. Ltd. (VCIPL), incorporated in January 2002, is a subsidiary of the Verizon Group, headquartered in the United States. VCIPL obtained an Internet Service Provider (ISP) licence from the Department of Telecommunications (DoT) in May 2003 and commenced providing telecommunication services during FY 2002-03.

In January 2008, VCIPL obtained National Long Distance (NLD) and International Long Distance (ILD) licences from DoT. The Verizon Group operates on a Hub-and-Spoke model — Verizon US serves as the "Hub" (the primary entrepreneur handling global strategy, R&D, brand management, and network procurement), while operating companies like VCIPL function as "Spokes" — local delivery centres compensated under a Limited Risk Model (LRM).

Under the LRM, VCIPL was guaranteed the higher of:

  • 11% operating margin on net sales, or
  • 14% mark-up on value-added costs (VAE)

— whichever was greater. This structure was uniform across all Verizon operating companies globally.

Issue 1: Section 80-IA — NLD/ILD as a "New Undertaking"?

The Revenue's position was straightforward: VCIPL obtained NLD and ILD licences in 2008 — well after the sunset date of 31 March 2005 under Section 80-IA(4)(ii). The AO treated NLD/ILD services as a separate, independent undertaking and denied the deduction on profits attributable to those services.

The assessee argued that the NLD/ILD licences were obtained merely to enhance the quality and security of the existing ISP-based telecommunication services. No new undertaking came into existence — the same infrastructure, the same customers, the same fundamental service.

The Tribunal's Holding:

Following its own decisions in the assessee's case for AY 2010-11, 2011-12, 2013-14, and 2015-16, the Tribunal held that no new undertaking was established by virtue of the NLD/ILD licences. The nature of telecommunications services rendered prior to 31 March 2005 remained fundamentally unchanged. The proportionate disallowance of ₹1,01,21,551 under Section 80-IA was deleted.

"No new undertaking came into existence by virtue of the NLD/ILD licenses, and the nature of services that were being rendered prior to 31 March 2005, fundamentally remained unchanged even under the NLD/ILD licenses."

Issue 2: Section 40(a)(i) — Are Telecom Charges "Royalty"?

VCIPL paid ₹161.06 crores to MCI Communications Services Inc. (MCICS) and MCI International Inc. (MCII) — both US-based group companies — for telecommunication services rendered outside India. The AO disallowed this under Section 40(a)(i), holding that the payments constituted "royalty" under Section 9(1)(vi) as amended by Finance Act 2012 (with retrospective effect), and that TDS should have been deducted under Section 195.

The assessee relied on:

  • The Delhi High Court in DIT v. New Skies Satellites BV — data transmission services are not royalty
  • The Delhi High Court in Asia Satellite Telecommunications Co. Ltd. — same principle
  • The Supreme Court in Engineering Analysis Centre of Excellence v. CIT [2021] — retrospective amendments to Section 9(1)(vi) cannot create withholding obligations for past transactions
  • The ITAT's own orders in the assessee's case for AY 2010-11, 2011-12, 2013-14, and 2015-16

The Tribunal's Holding:

The Tribunal followed the jurisdictional Delhi High Court's settled position and its own prior orders. Telecom charges paid to foreign operators for data transmission are not in the nature of royalty under the India-US DTAA. Retrospective amendments to the Act cannot be read into the treaty. The disallowance of ₹161.06 crores was deleted in full.

"Data transmission services could not qualify as royalty in order to be taxed under the Act... even if there is an amendment brought in the statute, the same cannot be read into the treaty."

Issue 3: Double Disallowance Under Section 40(a)(ia)

The AO disallowed ₹2,42,300 on an ad hoc basis for alleged non-deduction of tax under Section 194C. The assessee pointed out that it had already suo motu disallowed this amount in its return of income.

The Tribunal's Holding:

The matter was remitted to the AO for verification. If the assessee's claim is correct (that the amount was already disallowed), it cannot be disallowed again. Allowed for statistical purposes.

Issue 4: Transfer Pricing — The LRM Battle

This was the centrepiece of the case. The TPO made an upward adjustment of ₹83.50 crores, rejecting the assessee's TNMM-based benchmarking and applying the "Other Method" under Rule 10AB.

The TPO's Approach

The TPO rejected VCIPL's characterisation as a limited-risk service provider. His core argument:

  • VCIPL held its own telecom licences, owned significant fixed assets, bore operational and regulatory risk
  • The LRM was essentially a mechanism to cap VCIPL's upside profits and shift residual returns to the US parent
  • Contractual risk allocation cannot override substance — if VCIPL bears real operational risk, it deserves the residual returns
  • The "Other Method" was invoked: no independent third party would agree to have its profits capped in this manner

The TPO characterised the LRM adjustment (the difference between actual profit and capped profit) as an international transaction under Section 92B and determined its arm's length price at NIL — resulting in the ₹83.50 crore adjustment.

The Assessee's Defence

  • TNMM with Berry Ratio (operating profit / value-added expenses) was the appropriate method for a service provider whose value contribution is proportional to operating costs, not sales
  • VCIPL's NCPM of 14.1% exceeded the comparable weighted average of 13.47% — proving arm's length pricing
  • The LRM was applied globally across all Verizon OpCos and accepted in their respective jurisdictions
  • The TPO had accepted the LRM in all prior years (AY 2008-09 to 2010-11) — consistency demands the same treatment
  • The TPO failed to bring any comparable uncontrolled transaction on record before applying the "Other Method" — violating Rule 10AB

The Tribunal's Analysis

The Tribunal engaged in a remarkably detailed analysis of the inter-company service agreement, the compensation mechanism, and the actual financial results. Key observations:

1. The Hub-and-Spoke model is genuine. VCIPL only "completes the communication loop" for services initiated outside India. It adds value through local infrastructure and value-added expenses, but the core business strategy, R&D, brand, and global network are controlled by Verizon US.

2. Berry Ratio is appropriate. The Tribunal held that since VCIPL's value contribution is proportional to its operating expenses (not to sales volume), and it does not perform significant manufacturing or entrepreneurial functions, the Berry Ratio is a valid PLI under TNMM for this case. The DR's reliance on Sumitomo Corporation was distinguished on facts.

3. The TPO's "Other Method" was flawed. The TPO failed to identify any comparable uncontrolled transaction — a mandatory requirement under Rule 10AB. Citing Johnson & Johnson (Bombay HC), Merck Limited (Bombay HC), and SABIC India (Delhi ITAT, upheld by HC), the Tribunal noted that ALP determination without comparable data is "ad hoc and arbitrary."

4. However — VCIPL was under-compensated. In a significant finding, the Tribunal observed that under the agreed LRM formula, VCIPL should have been compensated at the higher of 11% on net sales or 14% on VAE. In practice, VCIPL received only the 14% on VAE (₹19.50 crores), whereas 11% on net third-party sales would have yielded ₹43.31 crores.

The Tribunal's Holding:

The TP adjustment was partly allowed. The Tribunal directed the AO/TPO to restrict the adjustment to ₹23.81 crores (₹43.31 crores minus ₹19.50 crores) — representing the shortfall in VCIPL's compensation under its own agreed LRM formula. The full ₹83.50 crore adjustment was rejected.

"The LRM is not only accepted and adopted by Verizon group in India as well as across the globe, all the OpCos are remunerated similarly and the same is accepted in their respective tax jurisdictions. Therefore, we should accept the uniform method adopted by the group across the globe..."
"It cannot be a situation where the revenue cherry picks the results."

Key Takeaways for Practitioners

  1. LRM structures are defensible — but ensure the tested party is actually receiving compensation per the agreed formula. The Tribunal caught a gap between what was promised (higher of two metrics) and what was paid (only the lower metric).
  2. Berry Ratio has a place in telecom TP — where the tested party adds value proportional to operating costs and does not control sales or perform entrepreneurial functions.
  3. "Other Method" requires comparable data — the TPO cannot simply declare an ALP at NIL without identifying a single uncontrolled transaction for comparison. Rule 10AB is not a blank cheque.
  4. Consistency matters — where the Revenue accepted a business model in prior years without challenge, it cannot flip positions in subsequent years without changed circumstances. Radhaswami Satsang v. CIT (193 ITR 321) remains good law.
  5. Telecom charges ≠ Royalty — for payments under the India-US DTAA, the jurisdictional High Court position is settled. Retrospective domestic amendments do not alter treaty definitions. Engineering Analysis Centre (SC) reinforces this.
  6. Section 80-IA: substance over licence — obtaining a new regulatory licence (NLD/ILD) does not automatically create a "new undertaking" if the fundamental nature of services remains the same.

The Broader Picture

This order is significant because it validates — with detailed reasoning — the commercial reality of how multinational telecom groups structure their operations. The Tribunal did not blindly accept the LRM; it independently verified the compensation formula against the service agreement and found that VCIPL was actually under-compensated. That's a rare and nuanced finding.

For MNEs operating in India with similar hub-and-spoke structures, this order provides both comfort and a warning: your LRM will be scrutinised not just for whether it exists, but for whether it's being correctly applied — in your favour as much as the Revenue's.

Citation: Verizon Communications India Pvt. Ltd. v. Additional CIT, Special Range-9, New Delhi — ITA No. 442/DEL/2017 (AY 2012-13) — ITAT Delhi Bench "I" — Order dated 25 February 2026.
